1. Germany Doxes “UNKN,” Head of RU Ransomware Gangs REvil, GandCrab

A major cybercrime boss has just been unmasked. German authorities have publicly identified 31-year-old Russian national Daniil Maksimovich Shchukin as "UNKN," the leader behind the notorious REvil and GandCrab ransomware gangs.

This doxing is a bold move. It targets the mastermind of groups that pioneered "double extortion," causing tens of millions in damage. If you were ever a target of these gangs, the man calling the shots is no longer a ghost in the machine.

The veil of online anonymity has been ripped away for a top-tier cybercriminal. In a rare and aggressive public action, Germany's Federal Criminal Police (BKA) has named, shamed, and published mugshots of the man they say ran two of the most destructive ransomware operations in recent history.

The individual is 31-year-old Russian Daniil Maksimovich Shchukin, known online as "UNKN." Authorities allege he led the GandCrab and REvil ransomware gangs, which were infamous for their "double extortion" tactics. They didn't just lock data—they also stole and threatened to publish it, squeezing victims for maximum payout.

The impact was severe and widespread. The BKA links Shchukin directly to at least 130 acts of sabotage and extortion in Germany alone between 2019 and 2021. These attacks caused over 35 million euros in economic damage, netting nearly 2 million euros in ransom payments from two dozen incidents.

Technically, Shchukin wasn't just a user but an architect. He operated the ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS) platforms that empowered other hackers (affiliates) to launch attacks using his tools. This model scaled the chaos, with REvil demanding ransoms in the tens of millions from major corporations like meat processor JBS.

So, what can potential victims do? While Shchukin's identification doesn't patch a specific vulnerability, it reinforces critical defenses. Robust, offline backups remain the ultimate shield against encryption-based extortion. Segmenting networks can limit the blast radius of an initial breach.

This public doxing is a significant shift in strategy. It moves beyond indictments that may never see a day in court and applies real-world pressure. By exposing his identity and linking him to a specific cryptocurrency wallet (holding over $317,000), authorities aim to disrupt his operations and travel.

In the bigger picture, this action signals that law enforcement is pursuing not just the malware, but the people who profit from it. It chips away at the perceived impunity of ransomware kingpins operating from safe havens. For the cybersecurity community, it's a rare and satisfying glimpse at the face behind some of the darkest digital threats of the past decade.

2. Hackers exploiting Acrobat Reader zero-day flaw since December

A silent, months-long campaign has been targeting Adobe Reader users through a dangerous zero-day flaw. Attackers are sending malicious PDFs that, once opened, can steal sensitive data and potentially hand over full control of your computer—all without a single click.

This isn't just another bug. It’s a highly sophisticated exploit that has been flying under the radar since December. If you use Adobe Reader to open documents from emails or unknown sources, you are directly in the crosshairs.

For at least four months, a stealthy hacking operation has been exploiting a critical, unpatched vulnerability in Adobe Reader. Security researcher Haifei Li of EXPMON discovered that simply opening a malicious PDF is enough to trigger the attack.

The exploit requires no user interaction beyond viewing the document. This makes it incredibly deceptive and effective. Once activated, it acts like a digital fingerprinting tool, silently harvesting data from the victim's system.

The attackers are using privileged Adobe APIs—specifically `util.readFileIntoStream` and `RSS.addFeed`—to steal local files and information. Worse, this initial breach is likely just the first step. Researchers warn it could pave the way for full Remote Code Execution (RCE), giving hackers complete control.

The campaign uses PDFs with Russian-language lures, referencing the oil and gas industry. This suggests a targeted, espionage-motivated operation, though the flaw itself threatens all Adobe Reader users globally.

Technically, this is a "zero-click" exploit in the wild. It works on the latest version of the software, bypassing all current defenses. The malware communicates outbound using HTTP requests with a tell-tale "Adobe Synchronizer" string in the User-Agent header.

Until Adobe releases a patch, the advice is stark: do not open PDFs from untrusted or unexpected sources. For network defenders, monitoring and blocking traffic containing that specific User-Agent string is a crucial temporary mitigation.

This incident is a stark reminder of how widely used software like Adobe Reader remains a prime target. A single flaw can serve as a silent gateway for months of unchecked data theft and system compromise.

It underscores the critical need for layered security. Beyond waiting for vendor patches, vigilance with email attachments and proactive network monitoring are essential shields against these sophisticated, patient attacks.

3. Eurail says December data breach impacts 300,000 individuals

A major breach at European rail travel giant Eurail has exposed the sensitive data of over 300,000 travelers. Attackers stole a treasure trove of personal information, including passport details and bank account numbers, later posting samples online for sale.

This isn't just about spam. For hundreds of thousands, especially young travelers in the EU's DiscoverEU program, this breach significantly elevates the risk of identity theft and sophisticated financial fraud. If you've used a Eurail or Interrail pass recently, your digital identity may be on the line.

The digital tracks have been compromised. Eurail B.V., the company behind popular Interrail and Eurail passes, confirmed a severe data breach from late December 2025, impacting a staggering 308,777 individuals. This incident exposes the vulnerabilities in systems that facilitate seamless travel across Europe.

So, what was stolen? The loot is alarmingly comprehensive. The unauthorized actors made off with full names, passport numbers, national ID details, and contact information like email and phone numbers. Crucially, for many, bank account IBANs and even health information were also exposed.

This is particularly concerning for young Europeans. Those who obtained passes through the EU's DiscoverEU initiative may have had even more sensitive data compromised, according to a separate alert from the European Commission. The breach paints a target on a highly mobile demographic.

The attackers didn't just steal and hide. They quickly operationalized the data, publishing a sample on Telegram and offering the full dataset for sale on the dark web. This move transforms a confidentiality failure into an active, ongoing threat for every affected individual.

Technically, the breach was a straightforward data exfiltration. On December 26, 2025, an unauthorized actor simply transferred files out of Eurail's network. The company took two months to fully review the files and understand the precise scope of the damage, a timeline that highlights response challenges.

For those affected, immediate action is required. Eurail advises updating your Rail Planner app password immediately—and crucially, not reusing that password anywhere else. Vigilance against phishing emails or calls referencing your travel details is now a permanent necessity.

You must also monitor your financial statements closely. While Eurail states no direct payment card data was stored, the exposed IBANs mean you should scrutinize your linked bank accounts for any unauthorized transactions and report anomalies instantly.

In the broader landscape, this breach is part of a worrying trend targeting European travel and institutional platforms. It follows closely on the heels of the European Commission's own *Europa.eu* hack, claimed by the ShinyHunters gang, signaling a focused assault on continental infrastructure.

Ultimately, this incident is a stark reminder. Even trusted services that facilitate life's enriching experiences, like European rail travel, are prime targets. It underscores a universal truth: in our interconnected world, the security of your personal data is only as strong as the weakest link in any organization you entrust it to.

4. Adobe Reader Zero-Day Exploited via Malicious PDFs Since December 2025

A previously unknown zero-day vulnerability in Adobe Reader has been actively exploited since at least December 2025. Attackers are using malicious PDFs, disguised as invoices, to steal sensitive data and potentially take full control of victims' systems.

This is a critical threat for anyone using Adobe Reader on Windows. The exploit works on the latest software version, bypassing all current defenses. If you open a rigged PDF, your data could be silently harvested before you even realize something is wrong.

A sophisticated cyber-espionage campaign has been quietly running for months, targeting users through a fundamental tool: Adobe Reader. Researchers have uncovered a zero-day flaw being exploited via malicious PDF documents, turning a simple file viewer into a powerful data theft engine.

The attack begins with a cleverly disguised PDF, often named something like "Invoice540.pdf." This social engineering lure, containing Russian-language text about the oil and gas industry, is designed to trick specific targets into opening it. Once launched in Adobe Reader, the trap is sprung.

The malicious file automatically executes obfuscated JavaScript. This code abuses an unpatched vulnerability to run privileged Acrobat APIs. In simple terms, it gives the attackers a high level of access within the application that should never be granted by a document.

This access is immediately used for information harvesting. The script acts as a sophisticated spy, collecting various types of sensitive local data from the infected machine. All stolen information is then sent to a remote attacker-controlled server.

The campaign's true danger lies in its potential. This initial data theft is likely just the first stage. The malware is designed to call back to its command server to receive additional JavaScript payloads.

These follow-on payloads could enable remote code execution (taking full control of the PC) or a sandbox escape (breaking out of security containers). While the final payload wasn't delivered in observed tests, the framework for a major breach is fully in place.

**Who is at risk?** Any Windows user running the latest version of Adobe Reader is vulnerable until a patch is released. The use of industry-specific lures suggests a targeted element, potentially focusing on entities within or connected to the Russian energy sector.

**What should you do?** Exercise extreme caution with PDFs, especially unexpected invoices or documents received via email. Consider using alternative PDF readers until Adobe issues an official security update. Organizations should monitor network traffic for connections to the suspicious IP address mentioned in reports.

This incident is a stark reminder that even ubiquitous, trusted software can harbor critical flaws. It underscores the relentless evolution of threats that combine technical zero-days with psychological social engineering, making vigilance and prompt patching non-negotiable.

5. ‘CanisterWorm’ Springs Wiper Attack Targeting Iran

A new cyber threat is weaponizing the cloud itself. A financially motivated group called TeamPCP has unleashed a self-spreading worm, dubbed 'CanisterWorm,' that targets poorly secured cloud services. But this isn't just another data theft scam.

The attack now includes a dangerous new twist: a data-wiping function specifically for systems in Iran. This escalates a criminal extortion campaign into a potentially destructive cyber conflict, putting any organization with exposed cloud infrastructure at immediate risk.

A cloud-native criminal operation has taken a sharp turn toward geopolitical disruption. The group known as TeamPCP, previously focused on data theft and extortion, has now deployed a worm with a built-in wiper module.

This new payload, 'CanisterWorm,' actively seeks out and destroys data on systems configured with Iran's time zone or Farsi language settings. It marks a significant escalation, blending financial crime with targeted destructive attacks.

The primary victims are organizations with misconfigured or exposed cloud services. TeamPCP doesn't rely on fancy new exploits. Their strength is in ruthless automation, scanning for and weaponizing known vulnerabilities in services like Docker, Kubernetes, and Redis.

They turn these cloud control planes into a self-propagating criminal ecosystem. Once inside, they steal credentials, move laterally, and now, deploy their politically motivated wiper.

The real-world impact is twofold. First, it creates collateral damage across global cloud infrastructure. Second, it demonstrates how criminal tools can be rapidly repurposed for state-aligned disruptive attacks, blurring the lines between cybercrime and hacktivism.

Technically, the worm spreads by exploiting publicly accessible APIs and known flaws. The wiper component is triggered by simple system settings—time zone and language—making it a crude but effective targeting mechanism.

For mitigation, the advice is urgent and clear. Security teams must immediately audit their cloud environments. Check for exposed APIs, enforce strict access controls, and apply patches for known vulnerabilities in Redis, Docker, and Kubernetes.

This incident is a stark lesson in the bigger cybersecurity landscape. It shows that unsecured cloud infrastructure isn't just a data risk; it can be hijacked as a weapon. The automation of old attacks creates new, scalable threats that can pivot from theft to destruction overnight.

The cloud's shared responsibility model is being tested. When criminal groups industrialize common misconfigurations, every exposed server becomes a potential launchpad for the next wave of attacks.
