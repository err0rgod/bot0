1. Google Blocks 8.3B Policy-Violating Ads in 2025, Launches Android 17 Privacy Overhaul

Google just dropped a massive privacy upgrade for Android and a staggering enforcement report. In 2025, they blocked a mind-boggling 8.3 billion policy-violating ads and suspended nearly 25 million accounts.

The real headline? Android 17 is getting a privacy overhaul that fundamentally changes how apps access your contacts and location. If you use Android, this is a direct win for your personal data.

Google’s latest announcements paint a dual picture: a relentless fight against digital fraud and a proactive shift towards stronger user privacy. The scale is almost incomprehensible—billions of bad ads blocked and millions of accounts shut down.

At the heart of the privacy push is a critical change in Android 17. For years, apps asking to "read your contacts" got access to *everything*. It was an all-or-nothing permission that exposed your entire address book.

That blanket access is now history. The new "Contact Picker" acts as a secure, standardized middleman. Instead of handing over the keys to your contacts, you now choose specific individuals to share.

Think of it like a bouncer at a club. Before, the app got the whole guest list. Now, you point out exactly who gets in. Apps must also declare precisely which data fields they need, like just a phone number, not every note and address.

This policy isn't a suggestion; it's a new rule. Apps targeting Android 17 must use this picker system. The old, broad `READ_CONTACTS` permission is being relegated only for essential apps that truly can't function without full access.

The motivation behind this is clear. Bad actors are weaponizing generative AI to create deceptive ads at an industrial scale. Google is countering with its own AI, Gemini, to detect these fakes in real-time.

This represents a significant move from reactive blocking to proactive prevention. The goal is to stop harmful content before it ever sees the light of day, a capability now expanding across ad formats.

For the average user, the Android change is a quiet but powerful victory. It minimizes your "permission footprint" and puts you in control. You no longer have to trust an app with all your data just to use one feature.

For developers, it's a call to action. App manifests need updating, and data collection practices must be refined to respect this new, granular model. It’s a necessary step towards building user trust.

Ultimately, this two-pronged approach—aggressive enforcement and architectural privacy—shows the modern cybersecurity playbook. You must both dismantle today's threats and redesign systems to prevent tomorrow's. Android 17 is doing just that, one contact at a time.

2. Webinar: From phishing to fallout — Why MSPs must rethink both security and recovery

Phishing isn't just a nuisance anymore—it's the engine of modern cybercrime, and it's being supercharged by AI. Managed Service Providers (MSPs) and the businesses they protect are in the crosshairs of attacks that are now too sophisticated for traditional defenses alone.

The fallout is severe. A single missed phishing email can now lead to a domino effect of data theft, ransomware, and crippling downtime. This upcoming webinar reveals why a security-only mindset is a recipe for disaster and how integrating recovery planning is the new imperative for survival.

The threat landscape has shifted. Cyberattacks are no longer just about breaking in; they're about a calculated journey from initial access to maximum damage. AI-powered phishing campaigns are crafting deceptive emails that easily slip past standard filters, making them a primary gateway for criminals.

These attacks are highly targeted, often focusing on MSPs themselves as a trusted gateway to their clients' networks. Once inside, adversaries don't just linger—they move laterally, exploiting trusted systems and SaaS platforms to steal data and deploy ransomware.

The real-world consequence is a brutal one-two punch: a security breach followed by a business continuity crisis. Many organizations find their defenses are only half the battle. Without a robust recovery plan, identifying a threat doesn't prevent weeks of costly downtime.

Technically, the failure point is often the gap between detection and response. Modern attacks are designed to bypass isolated security controls. When a breach occurs, delays in containment or inadequate, untested backups turn a manageable incident into a full-scale operational meltdown.

So, what must be done? MSPs and their clients need to fuse security with recovery into a single "cyber resilience" strategy. This means assuming breaches will happen and preparing accordingly. Critical steps include implementing immutable, offsite backups for all SaaS and on-premise data and rigorously testing disaster recovery plans.

The bigger picture is clear: prevention is vital, but it's no longer sufficient. In today's environment, your ability to recover swiftly and completely is just as critical as your ability to detect a threat. This integrated approach is what separates those who survive an attack from those who are defined by it.

This upcoming webinar, featuring experts from Kaseya, will dissect these evolving attack chains. It promises practical insights on closing the gaps in post-compromise response and building a security posture where recovery is not an afterthought, but a core pillar of your defense.

3. Shared Dictionaries: compression that keeps up with the agentic web

The web is getting heavier and more automated, creating a perfect storm for slower load times and ballooning bandwidth costs. A surge in AI agents and rapid development cycles means more data is being sent, more often, to both human visitors and automated bots.

The solution? A new "shared dictionary" compression technology. It promises to slash data transfer by sending only the *changes* to files a browser already has. This isn't just a performance tweak—it's a necessary upgrade for a web increasingly built and consumed by machines.

The modern web is caught in a squeeze. Pages grow 6-9% heavier each year, packed with more interactive code and media. At the same time, a new type of visitor is exploding in numbers: AI agents.

These automated bots crawl sites relentlessly, now making up nearly 10% of all web traffic. They fetch full pages repeatedly, often just to extract a tiny piece of data. This creates a massive redundancy problem.

Every request sends the same bulky files over and over. The strain isn't just on your site's speed, but on your infrastructure bill. More data transfer means higher costs.

The core issue is that traditional caching breaks down. AI-assisted development lets teams ship code updates faster than ever. But each new "deploy" can invalidate old caches, forcing every visitor—human or bot—to re-download everything.

Enter Shared Compression Dictionaries. Think of it as a smarter, collaborative form of compression. Instead of sending entire updated files, the server and browser work from a shared "dictionary" of common data.

The browser says, "Here's what I already have cached." The server then sends only the *differences*, or deltas, needed to update those files. It’s like getting a document edit history instead of a whole new copy.

For website owners, this means dramatically smaller payloads. Early tests show significant reductions in bytes transferred, especially for returning users. Pages load faster, and bandwidth costs drop.

The impact is twofold. Human visitors get a snappier experience. And the growing army of AI agents hits your servers less heavily, making your site more resilient and economical to operate.

Cloudflare is pioneering the infrastructure to make this standardized tech work at scale. Their beta opens on April 30, 2026, marking a crucial step.

This matters because it's a fundamental shift. We're not just optimizing for human patience anymore. We're building a web that can efficiently sustain its own automated, intelligent future. The goal is a faster, cheaper web for everyone—and everything—that uses it.

4. Three Microsoft Defender Zero-Days Actively Exploited; Two Still Unpatched

A security alarm is blaring for Microsoft Defender users. Threat actors are actively exploiting three zero-day vulnerabilities in the very software meant to protect Windows systems. Two of these critical flaws remain unpatched by Microsoft.

This means attackers can hijack Defender to gain elevated system privileges, disable updates, and move freely through a network. Any organization or individual relying on Microsoft Defender for endpoint security is potentially at immediate risk until fixes are deployed.

The security community is facing a tense scenario: the exploitation of zero-day flaws in Microsoft Defender, the built-in antivirus for Windows. Researchers at Huntress have caught threat actors actively using three vulnerabilities—BlueHammer, RedSun, and UnDefend—in real-world attacks.

These aren't just theoretical risks. Attackers are using them to escalate privileges after an initial breach. They gain higher-level system access, disable Defender's update mechanism, and execute hands-on-keyboard commands to explore and expand their control.

The situation is compounded by Microsoft's patch status. Only one of the three flaws, BlueHammer (CVE-2026-33825), received a fix in the latest Patch Tuesday. As of now, RedSun and UnDefend have no official patches, leaving systems exposed.

Technically, BlueHammer and RedSun are local privilege escalation (LPE) bugs within Defender. UnDefend is a denial-of-service flaw that blocks definition updates. This one-two punch lets attackers first gain higher privileges, then cripple the antivirus's ability to detect new threats.

The exploits were released publicly by a researcher known as Chaotic Eclipse, reportedly frustrated with Microsoft's disclosure process. This public PoC release has rapidly armed adversaries, with observed attacks starting as early as April 10th.

So, what should you do? First, ensure all systems have the April 2026 Patch Tuesday updates installed to mitigate BlueHammer. For the unpatched flaws, vigilance is key. Monitor for suspicious Defender-related processes and commands like `whoami /priv` or `net group` following an alert.

Consider layering your defenses. Don't rely solely on Defender. Use endpoint detection and response (EDR) tools that can spot the post-exploitation behavior these exploits enable, such as unusual privilege changes or update failures.

This incident matters profoundly. It undermines trust in a core, ubiquitous security component. When the guard itself can be weaponized, it forces a rethink of security architecture. It highlights the critical need for defense-in-depth and the real-world dangers of vulnerability disclosure disputes.

5. Unweight: how we compressed an LLM 22% without sacrificing quality

Cloudflare just turbocharged AI performance with a clever new trick. They've developed a system called "Unweight" that compresses the core data of large language models (LLMs) by up to 22% without losing any accuracy.

This isn't just a storage win—it's a speed breakthrough. By making models leaner, they dramatically reduce a critical memory bottleneck, slashing latency and cost for AI inference. Anyone using AI-powered services, especially through platforms like Cloudflare's network, could soon see faster, more responsive applications.

The race for faster, cheaper AI just hit a new milestone. Cloudflare's engineering team has unveiled "Unweight," a lossless compression system designed to shrink the massive data files—called model weights—that power large language models like the ones behind chatbots and copilots.

For AI providers, these models are notoriously hungry for GPU memory. The real bottleneck isn't raw computing power, but the speed at which data can be fed to the processors. Unweight tackles this by compressing weights by 15-22%, making every byte that travels from memory to processor count for more.

The magic isn't just in making files smaller on disk. The core innovation is *where* the decompression happens. Unweight decompresses the weights directly in the GPU's ultra-fast on-chip memory, bypassing a slower trip through the main memory. This turns a storage advantage into a massive speed boost.

Technically, it's a software-based solution requiring no special hardware. An intelligent runtime automatically selects the best execution strategy for each part of the model and workload, ensuring optimal performance whether you're processing a single query or a large batch.

So, what's the immediate impact? For developers and companies using Cloudflare's Workers AI platform, this translates to lower latency and reduced inference costs. End-users get snappier, more responsive AI features in the applications they use daily.

Looking ahead, this compression paradigm is a big deal. It paves the way for more efficient deployment of even larger, more complex models—like Mixture-of-Experts architectures—where fetching different model parts on demand is costly. Efficiency gains here directly lower the barrier to powerful AI.

In a generous move for the community, Cloudflare is open-sourcing Unweight. This invites broader research and collaboration, pushing the entire field toward a future where advanced AI is not only more capable but also fundamentally more efficient and accessible to all.

- NIST Limits CVE Enrichment After 263% Surge in Vulnerability Submissions: Imagine a flood of digital weak spots, so overwhelming that the nation's top vulnerability librarians are changing the rules. That's the reality at NIST, which runs the critical National Vulnerability Database (NVD). They've been swamped by a 263% surge in reported software flaws since 2020.

To manage this deluge, NIST is now forced to triage. Starting this month, they will only "enrich" a select group of vulnerabilities with crucial analysis and context. The rest will be listed but left bare, like a book in a library without a summary. This is a fundamental shift in how we understand digital risk.

So, which flaws get the special attention? NIST is prioritizing three key categories. First, any vulnerability already being actively exploited by hackers, as listed by CISA. Second, flaws in software used by the federal government. And third, bugs in "critical software" that controls essential systems.

This means a vast number of newly reported vulnerabilities will now be marked "Not Scheduled." For security teams everywhere, the noise just got louder. The burden of analysis is shifting from the central database to the individual organization, demanding more internal expertise and effort.

The immediate takeaway is clear: you cannot rely on the NVD alone anymore. Your first stop must be the CISA Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog—that's your non-negotiable patch list. Then, you must sharpen your own processes to assess which unscheduled flaws truly matter to your unique digital environment.

As one expert put it, this disruption ultimately matures the industry. It pushes us to prioritize *actual* exposure over *theoretical* severity. In an age of infinite vulnerabilities, a curated list of actionable threats is far more powerful for building resilience than a comprehensive but unmanageable archive of every minor bug.
- Apache ActiveMQ CVE-2026-34197 Added to CISA KEV Amid Active Exploitation: A digital ghost from the past has just been spotted in the wild. A critical vulnerability, hidden within the popular Apache ActiveMQ message broker for over a decade, is now being actively exploited by attackers. This flaw, known as CVE-2026-34197, is a ticking time bomb that allows intruders to run their own malicious code on vulnerable systems.

Think of it as a secret backdoor left slightly ajar. Attackers can exploit a management feature called the Jolokia API. By sending a specially crafted request, they can trick the system into fetching a harmful file from the internet and executing its commands. The lock on this door is also worryingly weak.

In many setups, the system still uses the factory-default username and password, which are easily guessed. Even worse, on certain versions, no password is required at all due to a separate oversight. This turns the flaw into a free pass for uninvited guests to take full control.

This threat casts a wide net, impacting a significant range of ActiveMQ Classic deployments. Specifically, any installation using a broker version older than 5.19.4 or versions 6.0.0 through 6.1.1 is at immediate risk. Security telemetry has already detected a sharp spike in exploitation attempts, proving attackers are moving fast.

The urgency is real because ActiveMQ is often the central nervous system for enterprise data. A breach here could lead to stolen information, crippled services, or provide a launchpad for attackers to spread deeper into a network. Its popularity has made it a repeated target for major cyber campaigns in recent years.

So, what’s the immediate play? Your first and most critical action is to patch. All affected systems must be upgraded to version 5.19.4 or 6.2.3 without delay. This is not a suggestion; for U.S. federal agencies, it’s now a mandated directive with a firm deadline.

Beyond patching, a thorough audit is essential. You need to hunt down any ActiveMQ instances with their Jolokia management interface exposed to the internet. Wherever possible, restrict access to this interface to trusted internal networks only and enforce strong, unique passwords.

Finally, ask the fundamental question: do you even need this management endpoint enabled? If the Jolokia service isn’t strictly required for your operations, disabling it entirely is one of the most effective ways to slam this backdoor shut for good. In today's landscape, closing these gaps before attackers find them is the only sustainable defense.

- CISA flags Apache ActiveMQ flaw as actively exploited in attacks: A digital ghost from the past has just been spotted in the wild. Cybersecurity authorities are sounding the alarm about a dangerous flaw in a widely used piece of internet plumbing called Apache ActiveMQ. This isn't just a theoretical risk—it's being actively exploited right now.

The vulnerability, hidden in the code for over 13 years, is a serious one. It allows attackers who have gained a foothold to inject malicious commands. Essentially, they can remotely take control of the entire server. This turns a trusted messaging system into a powerful weapon for hackers.

Who needs to worry? Any organization using unpatched versions of ActiveMQ Classic is in the crosshairs. Researchers have already identified more than 7,500 of these servers directly exposed on the open internet. The U.S. government has given its agencies a strict two-week deadline to fix the issue, highlighting the immediate danger.

The urgency is clear because ActiveMQ is a favorite target for ransomware groups and other malicious actors. Its common role in connecting critical applications makes it a perfect springboard for deeper network invasion. Past flaws in this same software have led to devastating breaches.

So, what's the action plan? If your team runs ActiveMQ, this is a top-priority patch. Immediately update to the fixed versions: 6.2.3 or 5.19.4. You can't afford to wait. For federal agencies, this directive is not a suggestion—it's a binding order with a hard deadline.

Security teams should also hunt for signs of compromise. Check your broker logs for suspicious connection attempts using specific technical parameters. Vigilance is key. If patching isn't possible right away, consider taking affected systems offline until you can secure them.

This alert is a stark reminder that old code can harbor new threats. The discovery of this flaw, aided by an AI tool, shows how the attack landscape is evolving. Defenders must move just as quickly. Prioritize this update to close a door that hackers are already trying to open.

- Microsoft: Some Windows servers enter reboot loops after April patches: Picture a critical server, the digital heartbeat of a corporate network, stuck in a nightmare loop. It boots up, tries to do its job, and then crashes—only to reboot and crash again. This is the disruptive reality Microsoft has confirmed for some organizations after applying their latest security patches.

The culprit is a crash in a core Windows security process, LSASS, which handles authentication. When this fails during startup, the entire server can spiral into a restart loop. The impact is severe: domain controllers become unavailable, locking users out and grinding directory services to a halt.

But here’s the crucial insight: this isn't a widespread consumer panic. The issue specifically targets *Windows Server domain controllers* in environments using a security feature called Privileged Access Management (PAM). If your organization uses PAM, servers running versions from 2016 to the latest 2025 could be vulnerable.

For the average personal computer, there’s no threat. This is a specialized scenario affecting managed IT infrastructure. However, for the sysadmins in those trenches, it’s a major operational headache that can render an entire domain inaccessible.

Microsoft is actively working on a resolution, but a formal fix isn’t ready yet. So, what’s the immediate takeaway? **Do not go it alone.** If you manage Windows servers and use PAM, you must contact Microsoft Support for Business *before* applying the April 2026 update, or immediately if you’re already seeing issues.

They can provide specific mitigation steps to break the reboot cycle and restore stability. This advisory highlights a recurring tension: the critical need for security patches versus their potential to disrupt the very systems they’re meant to protect. For now, the guidance is clear—prioritize a support call to navigate this patch safely.
